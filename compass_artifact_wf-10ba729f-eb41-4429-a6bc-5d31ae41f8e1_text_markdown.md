# A podcast road trip through the Deep South, curated for Helen

**These 22 episodes replace three true-crime podcasts with something better: movement history, cultural architecture, food as resistance, and voices that have earned the microphone.** Every recommendation below was cross-referenced for accuracy, framed as history or culture (never crime), and selected for the kind of meticulous production Helen demands. Each maps to a specific stop on the Birmingham → Mississippi → Houston route.

---

## BIRMINGHAM, ALABAMA

### The city's civil rights story told as movement history

**1. Uncivil — full series (especially "The Spin" and "The Raid")**
- **Podcast:** Uncivil (Gimlet Media)
- **Region:** Birmingham and the broader Alabama corridor
- **Apple Podcasts:** https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/uncivil/id1275078406
- **Length:** ~30 min per episode; 12 episodes total
- **Why it fits Helen:** This Peabody Award-winning series, named to TIME's 100 Best Podcasts of All Time, is co-hosted by journalist Jack Hitt and scholar Chenjerai Kumanyika. It dismantles the official version of the Civil War through stories of resistance, mutiny, and erasure — then connects those buried histories directly to present-day Southern politics. "The Spin" tackles Confederate monument controversies (acutely relevant as Helen passes through Birmingham, which fought Alabama's state government to remove its own monument). "The Raid" centers the agency of 250 formerly enslaved people who fought for their own liberation. The research is exhaustive, the tone is urgent without being sensational, and Kumanyika's presence ensures Black intellectual tradition anchors every episode.

**2. Code Switch — "Live From Birmingham…It's Code Switch!"**
- **Podcast:** Code Switch (NPR)
- **Episode:** "Live From Birmingham…It's Code Switch!" (August 2018)
- **Region:** Birmingham
- **Apple Podcasts:** https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/code-switch/id1112190608
- **Length:** ~45 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Recorded live at UAB's Alys Stephens Center, this episode features Mayor Randall Woodfin on growing up in the shadow of Birmingham's civil rights legacy, the stark economic divide between Birmingham and wealthy Mountain Brook, and what a post-industrial Southern city owes its Black residents. Poet Ashley M. Jones reads about learning to love her hometown. It's a portrait of a city actively reckoning with its past — exactly the context Helen needs driving in.

**3. Points South — "Stories of the South's Civil Rights Sites"**
- **Podcast:** Points South (Oxford American)
- **Region:** Birmingham
- **Apple Podcasts:** Search "Points South" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~30 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Producer Sara A. Lewis visits Birmingham and other key civil rights landmarks to examine how these sites tell their stories — who narrates, what gets preserved, what gets softened. The episode wrestles with the tension between memorialization and tourism, between dignity and spectacle. Beautifully scored original music. The Oxford American's editorial standards are exactly Helen's frequency: literary, rigorous, deeply Southern.

**4. 1619 — "The Economy That Slavery Built" (Episode 2)**
- **Podcast:** 1619 (The New York Times)
- **Episode:** Episode 2: "The Economy That Slavery Built"
- **Region:** Alabama corridor (contextual listening for the entire Deep South leg)
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available on Apple Podcasts under "1619"
- **Length:** ~40 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Nikole Hannah-Jones and sociologist Matthew Desmond trace how the cotton plantation economy of Alabama and Mississippi invented the management techniques, financial instruments, and labor exploitation that built American capitalism. This is essential intellectual context for driving through the Black Belt — the fertile crescent stretching from central Alabama into Mississippi where cotton was king and enslaved labor built everything Helen will see. The scholarship is rigorous (drawn from Desmond's Pulitzer-winning work) and the framing centers enslaved people as economic actors, not passive victims.

### Birmingham beyond the movement

**5. Code Switch — "The Story of Mine Mill" (December 2018)**
- **Podcast:** Code Switch (NPR)
- **Episode:** "The Story of Mine Mill"
- **Region:** Birmingham (Sloss Furnaces / industrial history)
- **Apple Podcasts:** https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/code-switch/id1112190608
- **Length:** ~35 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** This is the Sloss Furnaces episode Helen didn't know she needed. Reporter Julia Simon goes to Red Mountain to uncover the story of an interracial miners' union in Birmingham's steel industry — workers who organized across racial lines in the Jim Crow South, then watched their union get Red-baited and destroyed. Features Judge U.W. Clemon, Alabama's first Black federal judge. It connects Birmingham's industrial landscape directly to its civil rights history and centers working-class Black voices. Perfect companion for a Sloss Furnaces visit.

**6. Seizing Freedom — full series**
- **Podcast:** Seizing Freedom (VPM)
- **Region:** Alabama corridor
- **Apple Podcasts:** https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/seizing-freedom/id1520070952
- **Length:** ~25-30 min per episode; 28 episodes across 2 seasons
- **Why it fits Helen:** Hosted by historian Dr. Kidada E. Williams, this series uses first-person accounts from diaries, letters, and speeches — performed by voice actors — to reconstruct how Black people seized their own freedom from the Civil War through Jim Crow. Season 2 covers the era most relevant to Birmingham's story: Ida B. Wells's anti-lynching crusade, the rise of Black institutions, and resistance to racial terror across the Deep South. The scholarship is impeccable (Williams is a leading historian of racial violence), and the production treats every subject with the dignity of having their own words spoken aloud.

## McCOMB / JACKSON / VICKSBURG, MISSISSIPPI

### Mississippi's civil rights story as living history

**7. Radiolab — "The Flag and the Fury"**
- **Podcast:** Radiolab (WNYC Studios)
- **Episode:** "The Flag and the Fury" (July 2020)
- **Region:** Jackson, Mississippi (statewide)
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available on Apple Podcasts under "Radiolab"
- **Length:** ~50 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** duPont Award-winning reporting by Shima Oliaee and Jad Abumrad on Mississippi's state flag — the last in America to display the Confederate battle emblem. The episode tracks **126 years** of political maneuvering, features voices from inside the Mississippi Civil Rights Museum, and captures the 2020 moment when the flag finally came down. This is not a simple "progress narrative." It's an honest reckoning with how long white supremacy can be encoded in a symbol and defended by law. Exceptional sound design and the kind of patient, layered storytelling Radiolab does best.

**8. Points South — "Fannie Lou Hamer's Freedom Farm"**
- **Podcast:** Points South (Oxford American)
- **Episode:** "Fannie Lou Hamer's Freedom Farm"
- **Region:** Mississippi Delta / Sunflower County
- **Apple Podcasts:** Search "Points South" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~30 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Journalist Brittany Brown tells the story of Hamer's radical cooperative farming initiative in northern Mississippi — a project that sought food sovereignty for Black Delta communities locked out of the agricultural economy they had built with their own hands. This hits multiple notes Helen cares about: marginalized voices, food culture, movement history beyond the marches, and a Black woman's vision for economic self-determination. The Oxford American's production standards guarantee careful, literary storytelling.

**9. 99% Invisible — "America's Last Top Model"**
- **Podcast:** 99% Invisible
- **Episode:** "America's Last Top Model"
- **Region:** Jackson, Mississippi
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available on Apple Podcasts under "99% Invisible"
- **Length:** ~25 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** A perfect design-and-infrastructure episode for the architecture lover. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers built a **200-acre, three-dimensional physical model** of the entire Mississippi River Basin in Jackson, Mississippi — starting in 1943, partly using German POW labor. The model could simulate floods accurate to within inches. Now it sits abandoned and overgrown, with a grassroots effort to convert it into a public park. This is 99% Invisible at its best: a forgotten piece of infrastructure that reveals how humans tried to control the river that shaped the entire region Helen is driving through.

**10. Fresh Air — "For Jesmyn Ward, Writing Means Telling the 'Truth About the Place That I Live In'" (November 2017)**
- **Podcast:** Fresh Air (NPR)
- **Episode:** Interview with Jesmyn Ward about *Sing, Unburied, Sing*
- **Region:** Mississippi Gulf Coast / DeLisle / Parchman
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Fresh Air" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~35 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Ward discusses her second National Book Award-winning novel, set in a fictional Mississippi town modeled on her hometown of DeLisle. She talks about Parchman Prison's history, the weight of being a Black writer in Mississippi, and why she stays in a state that has tried to erase her community. Ward's voice — unflinching, deeply rooted, fiercely protective of her people — is exactly the literary companion Helen wants on this stretch of road. The interview is one of Terry Gross's finest late-career conversations.

**11. Washington Post "Constitutional" — "Fair Punishment" (Episode 9)**
- **Podcast:** Constitutional (The Washington Post)
- **Episode:** Episode 9: "Fair Punishment"
- **Region:** Parchman, Mississippi (Mississippi State Penitentiary)
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Constitutional" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~40 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Centers on Parchman Farm and the landmark case *Gates v. Collier* (1972), which exposed the prison's brutal conditions. Features civil rights attorney Roy Haber, who documented constitutional violations, and historian David Oshinsky (author of *Worse Than Slavery*). Crucially, the episode includes archival recordings from **Alan Lomax's Parchman field sessions** — work songs and spirituals performed by inmates that became foundational American music. This is history, not true crime: it examines how an institution of racial control was challenged through law and documented through music.

### Mississippi's cultural landscape

**12. Gravy — "Catfish Dream"**
- **Podcast:** Gravy (Southern Foodways Alliance)
- **Episode:** "Catfish Dream"
- **Region:** Mississippi Delta
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Gravy" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~20 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** The story of Ed Scott, who opened the **first Black-owned catfish processing plant** in the Mississippi Delta — a radical act in a region where Black farmers grew the catfish but white-owned companies controlled all the processing and profit. This is the Southern Foodways Alliance at its best: using food as a lens to examine race, economics, and resilience. Beautifully produced, centered entirely on a Black entrepreneur's voice.

**13. Scene on Radio — "Seeing White" (Season 2, especially Part 6)**
- **Podcast:** Scene on Radio
- **Episode:** "Seeing White" — 14-part series (Part 6 is Mississippi-focused)
- **Region:** Mississippi (and broader Deep South context)
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Scene on Radio" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~40-50 min per episode
- **Why it fits Helen:** Host John Biewen and collaborator Chenjerai Kumanyika (also of Uncivil) built one of the most acclaimed podcast series ever made about the construction of whiteness in America. Part 6 focuses specifically on Mississippi, examining how racial categories were invented, enforced, and naturalized. The series doesn't lecture; it genuinely investigates. Helen should listen to the full series before the trip if possible, but the Mississippi episode is essential context for driving through a state where these categories shaped every town, field, and courthouse she'll pass.

**14. 1619 — "The Land of Our Fathers" (Episode 5)**
- **Podcast:** 1619 (The New York Times)
- **Episode:** Episode 5: "The Land of Our Fathers"
- **Region:** Mississippi
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "1619" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~35 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Focuses on Black land loss in Mississippi — how families who acquired land after emancipation were systematically dispossessed through legal manipulation, violence, and government complicity. Features descendants of enslaved Mississippians fighting for what remains. This is not abstract history; it's about the specific dirt Helen is driving over and who was forced off it.

## HOUSTON, TEXAS

### Houston as America's most diverse city

**15. Gravy — "In Houston, Three Tastes of West Africa" (November 2022)**
- **Podcast:** Gravy (Southern Foodways Alliance)
- **Episode:** "In Houston, Three Tastes of West Africa"
- **Region:** Houston
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Gravy" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~25 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Reporter Kayla Stewart profiles three West African food establishments in Houston: Safari (opened by Nigerian immigrants in the 1990s), ChòpnBlọk (West African fast-casual by chef Ope Amosu), and Bludorn (where Senegalese-American GM Cherif Mbodji brings Senegalese flavors to fine dining). The episode contextualizes Houston's West African food scene within the **1965 Immigration Act** and Nigerian Civil War immigration patterns — connecting geopolitics to what's on the plate. Exactly the kind of immigrant-centered, deeply researched food story Helen craves.

**16. Gravy — "Brisket Pho, A Viet Tex Story"**
- **Podcast:** Gravy (Southern Foodways Alliance)
- **Episode:** "Brisket Pho, A Viet Tex Story"
- **Region:** Houston
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Gravy" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~25 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Explores the emergence of "Viet Tex" cuisine in Houston, featuring chef Don Nguyen of Khoi Barbecue and writer Dan Q. Dao. Houston is home to **140,000 Vietnamese residents** — the largest community outside Vietnam and Southern California — and this episode traces how Vietnamese refugees transformed Texas barbecue and crawfish culture from the inside. The reporting centers immigrant creativity and culinary genius, never exoticizing, always dignifying.

**17. 99% Invisible — "Missing the Bus"**
- **Podcast:** 99% Invisible
- **Episode:** "Missing the Bus"
- **Region:** Houston
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "99% Invisible" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~25 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Houston scrapped its entire bus network and redesigned it from scratch — a radical act for a sprawling, car-dependent city. Roman Mars interviews transit expert Steven Higashide about what happened and why other cities started copying Houston's approach. This is the design-systems thinking Helen loves: a city confronting its own infrastructure failures and choosing reinvention over incremental tinkering. A great companion to driving Houston's highways and understanding why the city looks and moves the way it does.

### Houston's hidden cultural layers

**18. Houston Matters — "Museum Visits: Project Row Houses" (July 2024)**
- **Podcast:** Houston Matters (Houston Public Media / KUHF)
- **Episode:** "Museum Visits: Project Row Houses"
- **Region:** Houston, Third Ward
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Houston Matters" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~15-20 min segment
- **Why it fits Helen:** Covers **Project Row Houses**, the 30-year experiment in socially engaged art where artist Rick Lowe and six other Black artists transformed abandoned shotgun houses in Houston's Third Ward into art installations, affordable housing, and community programs. Features curator Cydney Pickens and Ella Russell of Crumbville, TX Bakery. This is art as community infrastructure, architecture as activism — exactly the intersection of design, marginalized voices, and cultural dignity that defines Helen's interests.

**19. Below the Waterlines: Houston After Hurricane Harvey (Houston Public Media, 2022)**
- **Podcast:** Below the Waterlines (Houston Public Media)
- **Full series:** Multi-episode investigative podcast
- **Region:** Houston (Buffalo Bayou, flooding, water infrastructure)
- **Apple Podcasts:** Search "Below the Waterlines" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** Multiple episodes, ~25-35 min each
- **Why it fits Helen:** Investigative series examining Houston five years after Hurricane Harvey flooded **150,000+ homes** in Harris County. Episodes cover reservoir risks, flood mitigation engineering, green infrastructure experiments, potential tunnel systems, home buyout programs, and — crucially — the **racial and economic disparities** in who recovered and who didn't. This is Houston's relationship to water told with the rigor and structural analysis Helen demands. Perfect for understanding Buffalo Bayou as a living system, not just a park.

**20. Floodlines (The Atlantic, 2020)**
- **Podcast:** Floodlines (The Atlantic)
- **Full series:** 8 episodes
- **Region:** Gulf South corridor (New Orleans focus, Houston connections)
- **Apple Podcasts:** Available under "Floodlines" on Apple Podcasts
- **Length:** ~35-45 min per episode
- **Why it fits Helen:** Hosted by Vann R. Newkirk II, this is arguably the finest podcast ever made about an American disaster. While centered on Hurricane Katrina and New Orleans, the series is essential Gulf South listening that includes Houston's role as a destination for hundreds of thousands of Katrina evacuees. Newkirk — a Black journalist from the South — frames the catastrophe as a story of abandonment, resilience, and the political construction of "natural" disasters. The reporting won a Peabody nomination. Helen should listen to this across the Mississippi-to-Houston stretch as a bridge between the two states.

**21. NPR — "Saving Houston's LGBTQ History Through Thousands of Hours of Radio Archives" (June 2024)**
- **Podcast/Feature:** NPR (reported by Neda Ulaby)
- **Region:** Houston, Montrose neighborhood
- **Available on:** NPR.org and NPR podcast feeds
- **Length:** ~8 min
- **Why it fits Helen:** Reports on the University of Houston's digitization of **3,500+ files** of Houston LGBTQ radio programming from the 1970s through 2000s, including *Wilde 'n' Stein* (started 1975 on KPFT 90.1), *After Hours*, and 48 episodes of *TV Montrose*. Features remarkable archival audio: 1979 interviews with Black gay activists, coverage of early Pride parades, stories from the Montrose community. This is preservation of marginalized voices in its most literal form — perfect context before walking Montrose. Shorter than the other picks but dense with history.

**22. Mogul — Season 3: DJ Screw (Spotify)**
- **Podcast:** Mogul (Spotify Original, formerly Gimlet)
- **Season:** Season 3, dedicated to DJ Screw
- **Region:** Houston, Southside
- **Available on:** Spotify (exclusive)
- **Length:** Full season, multiple episodes
- **Why it fits Helen:** A narrative season dedicated to Robert Earl Davis Jr. — DJ Screw — who invented the chopped-and-screwed sound that became Houston's signature musical contribution to the world. The series traces how Screw's slowed-down mixtapes emerged from Houston's Southside and created an entirely new genre. This is a story about a Black artist who built a cultural movement from a bedroom in a neighborhood the mainstream music industry ignored. Note: Spotify exclusive, not on Apple Podcasts — but worth downloading for the Houston leg.

## How to sequence these for the drive

The road trip has a natural narrative arc, and these episodes can be layered to build on each other:

**Before departure:** Listen to *Seeing White* (Scene on Radio) and the *1619* episodes as foundational context. These will reframe everything Helen sees.

**Birmingham leg (Episodes 1–6):** Start with *Uncivil* episodes for Civil War context, then *Code Switch*'s Birmingham episodes for the contemporary city, then *Seizing Freedom* for the Reconstruction-to-Jim-Crow bridge. Save *Points South*'s civil rights sites episode for the morning Helen visits the Birmingham Civil Rights Institute.

**Mississippi leg (Episodes 7–14):** Open with *Radiolab*'s "The Flag and the Fury" for a visceral entry into Mississippi's racial politics. Listen to Jesmyn Ward's *Fresh Air* interview while driving through her landscape. Save *99% Invisible*'s "America's Last Top Model" for the approach to Jackson, and *Gravy*'s "Catfish Dream" for the Delta.

**Houston leg (Episodes 15–22):** Lead with both *Gravy* food episodes to understand Houston as immigrant city. Listen to *Missing the Bus* while stuck in Houston traffic. Save *Project Row Houses* for the day Helen visits Third Ward, and *Below the Waterlines* for any drive along Buffalo Bayou. End the trip with *Floodlines* — it connects everything about the Gulf South into a single devastating, beautiful narrative about what we build, what we lose, and who gets left behind.
